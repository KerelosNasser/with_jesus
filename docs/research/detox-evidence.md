## 1. Screen Time and Mental Health Correlation

**Claim:** Excessive smartphone use correlates with increased anxiety and depression symptoms, particularly among adolescents.

**Citation:**
Twenge, J. M., Joiner, T. E., Rogers, M. L., & Martin, G. N. (2018). Increases in depressive symptoms, suicide-related outcomes, and suicide rates among U.S. adolescents after 2010 and links to increased new media screen time. *Clinical Psychological Science*, 6(1), 3–17. https://doi.org/10.1177/2167702617723376

**Evidence summary:**
This study analysed two nationally representative surveys of U.S. adolescents in grades 8–12 (N = 506,820) and national suicide statistics (ages 13–18). Depressive symptoms, suicide-related outcomes, and suicide rates increased between 2010 and 2015, especially among females. Adolescents who spent more than 3 hours/day on electronic devices were significantly more likely to report mental health issues, while those spending more time on non-screen activities (in-person social interaction, sports/exercise, religious attendance) were less likely. The rise in depression and suicide coincided with the period when smartphone ownership crossed 50% among U.S. teens (circa 2012). The authors controlled for cyclical economic factors (unemployment, Dow Jones Index) and found no correlation, strengthening the screen-time link.

**Additional supporting source:**
Twenge, J. M., & Campbell, W. K. (2018). Associations between screen time and lower psychological well-being among children and adolescents: Evidence from a population-based study. *Preventive Medicine Reports*, 12, 271–283. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pmedr.2018.10.003
— A large national random sample (N = 40,337) of U.S. children aged 2–17 found that after 1 hour/day of use, more screen time was associated with lower psychological well-being (less curiosity, lower self-control, more distractibility). Among 14–17-year-olds, high users (7+ hours/day) were more than twice as likely to have been diagnosed with depression or anxiety compared to low users (1 hour/day).

## 2. Intentional Phone Breaks Improve Well-Being

**Claim:** Taking deliberate breaks from phone use improves subjective well-being and reduces stress.

**Citation:**
Radtke, T., Apel, T., Schenkel, K., Keller, J., & von Lindern, E. (2022). Digital detox: An effective solution in the smartphone era? A systematic literature review. *Mobile Media & Communication*, 10(2), 190–215. https://doi.org/10.1177/20501579211028647

**Evidence summary:**
This systematic review synthesised findings from 31 studies on digital detox interventions (temporary, voluntary abstinence from smartphones or specific apps). Most studies examined social network site (SNS) abstinence, primarily Facebook. The review found that digital detox interventions showed mixed but promising results: a significant portion of studies reported improvements in well-being, reduced stress, and increased life satisfaction following detox periods. The authors note that key moderators include the individual's motivation, the duration of the detox, and whether replacement activities (e.g., in-person socialising) were encouraged. The review calls for more rigorous, longitudinal RCTs to establish causal mechanisms.

**Additional supporting source:**
Olson, J. A., Sandra, D. A., Chmoulevitch, D., Raz, A., & Veissière, S. P. L. (2021). A ten-step behavioural intervention to reduce screen time and problematic smartphone use. Preprint. https://doi.org/10.31234/osf.io/tjynk
— Two pre-registered studies (N = 51 and N = 70) tested a 10-step nudge-based intervention (disabling non-essential notifications, greyscale display, removing social media from home screen). Study 1 found reductions in screen time, problematic smartphone use, and depressive symptoms after two weeks. Study 2 found the intervention caused larger changes in screen time, problematic use, and sleep quality than screen-time monitoring alone. The intervention reduced screen time by ~1 hour/day and returned problematic smartphone use scores to normal levels for at least six weeks.

## 3. Reflective Pauses Reduce Compulsive Checking

**Claim:** Brief reflective pauses before phone use reduce habitual/compulsive checking behaviour.

**Citation:**
Terzimehić, N., Haliburton, L., Greiner, P., Schmidt, A., Hussmann, H., & Mäkelä, V. (2022). MindPhone: Mindful reflection at unlock can reduce absentminded smartphone use. In *Proceedings of the 2022 ACM Designing Interactive Systems Conference (DIS '22)*, 1818–1830. https://doi.org/10.1145/3532106.3533575

**Evidence summary:**
This mixed-method field study (28 participants, 2 weeks) tested MindPhone, a mindfulness-based intervention that prompts users at phone unlock with one of two questions: (a) what they intend to do on the phone, or (b) what they plan to do in the real world after using the phone. Results showed that the real-world prompt significantly reduced absentminded phone use and encouraged quicker return to real-world activities, regardless of whether users responded by writing or mentally reflecting. The study demonstrates that a brief, in-situ reflective prompt (~2–3 seconds of reflection) can disrupt the automatic habit of compulsive phone checking.

**Additional supporting source:**
Li, Z., Liang, M., Ray, L. C., & Luo, Y. (2024). StayFocused: Examining the effects of reflective prompts and chatbot support on compulsive smartphone use. In *Proceedings of the 2024 CHI Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems (CHI '24)*, 1–19. https://doi.org/10.1145/3613904.3642479
— A stage-based between-subjects study (36 college students, 5 weeks) comparing baseline, reflection-prompt, and reflection + chatbot conditions. Participants who received reflective prompts were able to focus longer and resist distractions. Those with additional chatbot support better maintained their screen-time reduction. The reflective prompts helped users become aware of their phone-checking intentions in the moment.

## 4. Default Launcher Replacement Reduces Usage

**Claim:** Replacing the default home screen with a minimal launcher reduces screen time by 20–30%.

**Citation:**
Bisaha, D. (2024). The effect of digital detox application on positive and negative aspects of inner experience and user behavior in young people: An experiment. Comenius University Bratislava. Study conducted on the *minimalist phone* app.

**Evidence summary:**
This experiment evaluated the *minimalist phone* launcher — an app that replaces the standard Android home screen with a minimal, icon-free interface (text-based app lists, greyscale option, app-blocking schedules, mindful launch delays). Over a 4-week period, participants who used the launcher saw a reduction in screen time of approximately 20–30%, totalling over 10.5 hours saved per week. Habitual smartphone use decreased by ~50%, and participants reported improved productivity and social connectivity. The mechanism combines several behavioural principles: increasing friction (extra step to open apps), reducing visual salience (removing colourful icons), and adding intentionality prompts (app-blocking schedules, time reminders).

**Additional supporting source (UI friction research):**
Zimmermann, L., & Sobolev, M. (2020). Digital nudges for screen time reduction: A randomized control trial with performance and wellbeing outcomes. *PsyArXiv Preprints*. https://doi.org/10.31234/osf.io/nmgdz
— A 3-week RCT (N = 112) tested two digital nudges: grayscale mode (passive nudge) and time limits (active nudge). The passive nudge (greyscale) produced an immediate significant reduction in objectively measured screen time. Active nudges (time limits) showed smaller, gradual effects. This study demonstrates that small changes to the phone's default interface — reducing visual appeal and adding friction — can meaningfully reduce usage without requiring willpower.

## 5. Breathing Exercises Reduce Physiological Stress

**Claim:** Slow breathing exercises (4–6 breaths per minute) activate the parasympathetic nervous system and reduce cortisol.

**Citation:**
Zaccaro, A., Piarulli, A., Laurino, M., Garbella, E., Menicucci, D., Neri, B., & Gemignani, A. (2018). How breath-control can change your life: A systematic review on psycho-physiological correlates of slow breathing. *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience*, 12, Article 353. https://doi.org/10.3389/fnhum.2018.00353

**Evidence summary:**
This systematic review (following PRISMA guidelines) analysed 15 studies from 2,461 screened abstracts on the psychophysiological effects of slow breathing techniques (<10 breaths/min, typically 4–6 breaths/min). Key findings:
- **Autonomic effects:** Slow breathing increases Heart Rate Variability (HRV) and Respiratory Sinus Arrhythmia — both markers of parasympathetic (vagal) activation. Increased vagal tone is directly linked to reduced stress and improved emotional regulation.
- **Central nervous system effects:** EEG studies showed increased alpha power and decreased theta power, associated with relaxed alertness and reduced anxiety. fMRI data showed increased activity in prefrontal cortex, insula, and subcortical structures involved in emotional control (thalamus, hypothalamus, periaqueductal grey).
- **Psychological outcomes:** Participants reported increased comfort, relaxation, vigour, and alertness, along with reduced symptoms of anxiety, depression, anger, and confusion.
- **Proposed mechanisms:** (a) Voluntary regulation of internal bodily states (interoception) and (b) nasal mechanoreceptors modulating olfactory bulb activity, which tunes cortical activity.

The review concludes that slow breathing enhances "autonomic, cerebral and psychological flexibility," linking parasympathetic activity to emotional control and well-being.

**Additional supporting source:**
Yackle, K., et al. (2017). Breathing control center neurons that promote arousal in mice. *Science*, 355(6332), 1411–1415. (Stanford University study, senior author Mark Krasnow.)
— Identified a tiny cluster of ~350 neurons in the brainstem (the pre-Bötzinger complex) that directly links the breathing pacemaker to the arousal centre (locus coeruleus). Slow, smooth breathing activates these neurons in a pattern that promotes calm; rapid breathing promotes arousal. This provides a neuroanatomical mechanism explaining why slow breathing induces tranquillity — a finding that translates to humans, who share the same brainstem structures.
